**Authors:** Robert Folger and Mary A. Konovsky

**Published:** 1989, *Academy of Management Journal*, Vol. 32, No. 1, pp. 115–130

**Domain:** Employee reactions to pay raise decisions

Do employees care more about the size of their raise, or about how the raise decision was made? Folger and Konovsky surveyed 217 employees and found that the answer depends on what outcome you measure. Distributive justice (whether the raise itself seemed fair) was the stronger predictor of satisfaction with pay. But procedural justice (whether the process for determining raises seemed fair) was the stronger predictor of broader organizational attitudes — trust in supervisor, organizational commitment, and evaluation of the employer. Fair processes predicted loyalty; fair outcomes predicted satisfaction with the specific decision.

This distinction has become one of the most robust findings in organizational justice research. It means that an employee can be unhappy with their raise but still trust and feel committed to the organization if they believe the process was fair. Conversely, a generous raise delivered through an opaque or arbitrary process may satisfy in the short term but erode trust over time. For compensation and HR professionals, the implication is that the design and communication of pay processes matters at least as much as the pay numbers themselves.

This was one of the first empirical papers to demonstrate the independent effects of procedural and distributive justice on different classes of workplace outcomes.

## Background

The study builds on Leventhal's (1980) criteria for procedural fairness (consistency, bias suppression, accuracy, correctability, representativeness, ethicality) and applies them to a real pay raise context in a manufacturing organization.

## Key Arguments

| Justice Type | Strongest Predictor Of | Weaker Predictor Of |
|---|---|---|
| Distributive (raise amount fairness) | Pay satisfaction | Organizational commitment, trust |
| Procedural (process fairness) | Trust in supervisor, organizational commitment | Pay satisfaction |

The key insight: process and outcome fairness predict *different things* — they are not substitutes for each other.

## Open Questions

- Does this pattern hold for other compensation decisions (bonuses, equity grants, promotions)?
- How do transparent vs. opaque pay structures interact with procedural and distributive justice perceptions?
- In the age of pay transparency legislation, does the procedural-distributive distinction shift?
