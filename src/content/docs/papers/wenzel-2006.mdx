**Authors:** Michael Wenzel

**Published:** 2006, *Social Justice Research*, Vol. 19, pp. 345–364

**Domain:** Tax administration (Australian Taxation Office)

While much of the procedural justice literature relies on survey data and correlational analysis, Wenzel's 2006 study stands out for its use of a field experiment — one of the first in tax compliance research. The study tested whether reminder letters sent to 2,052 Australian taxpayers who had failed to file on time could improve compliance depending on how those letters were framed. Specifically, Wenzel compared a standard bureaucratic reminder against two letters designed to embody principles of informational justice (explaining the reasons behind the requirement) and interpersonal justice (treating the taxpayer with respect and dignity).

The results demonstrated that both fairness-oriented letters produced significantly higher filing rates than the standard control letter. This finding is important because it shows that procedural fairness is not merely a perception that correlates with compliance — it is a causal mechanism that can be deliberately engineered into government communications. The study moved the field beyond asking "do people who feel fairly treated comply more?" to "can we design fairer interactions that produce more compliance?"

The practical implications are considerable. If something as simple as the tone and content of a government letter can shift compliance behavior, then procedural justice is not an abstract ideal but a concrete design parameter for public administration.

## Background

Wenzel draws on Colquitt's (2001) four-factor model of organizational justice, which distinguishes between distributive, procedural, informational, and interpersonal justice. The study adapts these constructs from the workplace to the citizen-state relationship, testing whether the informational and interpersonal dimensions independently affect behavior in a regulatory context.

## Key Arguments

| Letter Type | Design Principle | Effect on Compliance |
|---|---|---|
| Standard reminder | Bureaucratic, neutral tone | Baseline compliance rate |
| Informational justice letter | Explained reasons and rationale for filing obligation | Significantly higher compliance than control |
| Interpersonal justice letter | Respectful tone, acknowledged taxpayer's perspective | Significantly higher compliance than control |

The key insight is that *how* the government communicates — not just *what* it demands — has measurable effects on citizen behavior.

## Open Questions

- Do these effects persist over time, or do they only shift immediate behavior?
- How do informational and interpersonal justice interact when both are present in the same communication?
- Would similar effects hold in higher-stakes regulatory contexts (e.g., environmental fines, immigration notices)?
