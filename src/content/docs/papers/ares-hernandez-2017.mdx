**Authors:** Macarena Ares and Enrique Hernández

**Published:** 2017, *Research & Politics*, Vol. 4, No. 2

**Domain:** Political trust and corruption scandals (Spain)

Does a corruption scandal *cause* trust to decline, or do people who already distrust politicians simply pay more attention to corruption? Ares and Hernández answer this question through a natural experiment: the Bárcenas scandal — involving secret payments to senior officials of Spain's ruling party — broke during the fieldwork period of the European Social Survey in Spain, creating a fortuitous before-and-after comparison. Citizens surveyed after the scandal broke reported substantially lower trust in politicians than those surveyed before, even though both groups were drawn from the same random sample. The effect was independent of partisan preferences: corruption damaged trust across the political spectrum, not just among supporters of the opposition.

The natural experiment design provides unusually strong causal evidence for what is often an observational claim. The finding that trust damage transcends partisanship is especially important — it means corruption is not filtered through partisan loyalty but registers as a more fundamental violation of the expectations citizens hold for their political institutions.

## Background

The Bárcenas scandal, which erupted in January 2013, involved the publication of secret ledgers showing undeclared payments to senior officials of Spain's Partido Popular (PP), then the governing party. The revelation coincided with the Spanish fieldwork wave of the European Social Survey (ESS), Round 6. Ares (University of Zurich) and Hernández (Universitat Autònoma de Barcelona) exploit this coincidence: by comparing ESS respondents interviewed before the scandal broke with those interviewed after, they create a quasi-experimental design. Because ESS sampling is random and the scandal's timing was exogenous to the survey schedule, the pre/post comparison captures the causal effect of the scandal on trust rather than pre-existing differences between respondents.

## Key Arguments

| Finding | Detail |
|---|---|
| Causal effect on trust | The Bárcenas scandal caused a significant decline in trust in politicians, not just a correlation |
| Non-partisan damage | The trust decline was not limited to opposition supporters — it affected citizens across the political spectrum |
| Immediate impact | The effect was strongest immediately after the scandal broke, demonstrating the rapid trust-destroying power of corruption revelations |
| Natural experiment design | The coincidence of the scandal with ESS fieldwork provides unusually clean identification |
| Fundamental violation | Corruption registers not as a partisan issue but as a breach of basic expectations about institutional conduct |

## Open Questions

- How long does the immediate trust shock persist — does trust gradually recover, or does the scandal produce a lasting shift?
- Do different types of corruption scandals (financial, sexual, abuse of power) produce different magnitudes of trust decline?
- Is the non-partisan nature of the trust effect specific to high-profile national scandals, or does it also hold for local corruption cases?
