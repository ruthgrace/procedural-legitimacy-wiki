**Authors:** Kenneth Eckstein

**Published:** 2022, *Administration & Society*, Vol. 54, No. 9, pp. 1707–1731

**Domain:** Bureaucratic discretion, deliberative democracy, administrative legitimacy

Eckstein offered a framework for when bureaucratic discretion can be considered legitimate. Drawing on deliberative democratic theory, he argued that discretion is legitimate when three conditions are met: it is exercised transparently (the decision-maker explains what they are doing and why), the reasons for decisions are articulated and subject to challenge (not hidden behind bureaucratic opacity), and affected parties have meaningful opportunities to contest discretionary outcomes (not just formal appeal rights that are practically inaccessible).

Under this framework, the problem is not discretion itself but unaccountable discretion — decisions made without explanation, review, or recourse. A building inspector who explains the basis for a citation and provides a clear process for appeal exercises legitimate discretion. One who cites selectively, provides no explanation, and offers no meaningful recourse exercises illegitimate discretion — even if both follow the same formal rules.

For procedural legitimacy, Eckstein provides a practical framework that bridges the literature on discretion with procedural justice theory. His conditions for legitimate discretion — transparency, reason-giving, and contestability — map directly onto core procedural justice principles. This suggests that discretion can be made legitimate through procedural design, without the need to eliminate flexibility.

## Background

Eckstein draws on Habermasian deliberative democratic theory to construct a normative framework for evaluating bureaucratic discretion. He applies this framework to case studies of discretionary decision-making in public administration to illustrate when discretion meets and fails to meet the conditions for legitimacy.

## Key Arguments

| Condition | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Transparency | Decision-makers must explain what they are doing and why |
| Reason-giving | Decisions must be based on articulated, challengeable reasons |
| Contestability | Affected parties must have meaningful opportunities to challenge outcomes |
| Accountability | Discretion without accountability is inherently illegitimate |
| Procedural design | These conditions can be built into institutional structures |

## Open Questions

- Can transparency and reason-giving requirements be implemented without creating excessive documentation burden?
- How meaningful must contestability be — is a formal appeal right sufficient, or must it be practically accessible?
- Does making discretion transparent change how it is exercised (a form of the observer effect)?
