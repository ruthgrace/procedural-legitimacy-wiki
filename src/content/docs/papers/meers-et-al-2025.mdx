**Authors:** Jed Meers, Aisling Ryan, and Joe Tomlinson

**Published:** 2025, *Journal of Law and Society*, Vol. 52, p. 81

**Domain:** Government application forms and bureaucratic processes (UK)

Government forms are among the most routine points of contact between citizens and the state — and among the least studied from a procedural justice perspective. Meers, Ryan, and Tomlinson ask a deceptively simple question: does giving applicants space to explain their circumstances on a government form increase their perception that the process is fair? Through a survey experiment with 655 recipients of Universal Credit in the UK, they find that it does. Including space for personal narrative — a free-text box where applicants can tell their story — significantly increases perceptions of procedural fairness compared to forms that only collect structured data.

This finding has striking implications for local government. Permit applications, variance requests, tax assessment appeals, business license renewals, and countless other municipal processes involve forms that typically collect only checkboxes and fill-in-the-blank data. The study suggests that something as simple as adding a "tell us about your situation" field could meaningfully improve how citizens experience these interactions. The mechanism is voice: procedural justice theory has long held that people want an opportunity to be heard, and a narrative field on a form provides that opportunity even in a fully bureaucratic, non-face-to-face process.

The paper also demonstrates that procedural fairness is not just a property of high-stakes proceedings like hearings or trials — it operates at the level of everyday administrative design. For local governments with limited resources, form redesign is one of the lowest-cost interventions available for improving perceived fairness.

## Background

The study is grounded in Thibaut and Walker's (1975) concept of "process control" (voice) and tests it in the specific context of bureaucratic form design. The UK Universal Credit system is a welfare benefit administered through an online application, making the findings directly relevant to any government application process.

## Key Arguments

| Form Design Element | Effect on Procedural Fairness |
|---|---|
| Space for personal narrative (free-text field) | Significantly increases perceived fairness |
| Structured data only (checkboxes, dropdowns) | Lower perceived fairness |
| Voice mechanism | The narrative field functions as a voice opportunity, even without a human interaction |

| Local Government Application | Potential for Narrative Field |
|---|---|
| Building permit application | Applicant explains the project's purpose and context |
| Zoning variance request | Applicant describes hardship or unique circumstances |
| Property tax assessment appeal | Homeowner explains why assessment seems incorrect |
| Business license application | Owner describes the business and community benefit |
| Code violation response | Resident explains circumstances and remediation plan |

## Open Questions

- Does the perceived fairness benefit persist if applicants learn that the narrative field was not actually read by a decision-maker?
- How should local governments train staff to meaningfully engage with narrative responses rather than ignoring them?
- Do narrative fields create equity concerns (favoring more articulate applicants) even as they improve perceived fairness?
