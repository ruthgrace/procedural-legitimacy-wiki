**Authors:** Joel Brockner and Batia M. Wiesenfeld

**Published:** 1996, *Psychological Bulletin*, Vol. 120, No. 2, pp. 189–208

**Domain:** Employee reactions to organizational decisions, with extensive application to layoffs and downsizing

When organizations lay people off, how do the survivors react? Brockner and Wiesenfeld's influential review of 45 independent samples revealed a crucial interaction: procedural justice matters most when outcomes are unfavorable. Employees who receive bad news through a fair process react much better than those who receive the same bad news through an unfair process. But when outcomes are favorable, procedural fairness has a weaker effect — people are less attentive to process when they're already getting what they want.

This interaction effect has become one of the most replicated findings in organizational justice research. It has profound implications for how organizations handle difficult decisions. During layoffs, restructurings, pay cuts, or benefit reductions — situations where outcomes are inherently negative for those affected — procedural fairness becomes the primary lever for maintaining trust, commitment, and cooperation among remaining employees. Organizations that cut corners on process precisely when things are hardest are making the worst possible mistake.

The paper also works in reverse: outcome fairness matters more when procedural justice is low. When processes are perceived as unfair, people scrutinize outcomes more carefully. This means that procedural and distributive justice serve as partial substitutes — each matters more when the other is lacking.

## Background

The review draws on studies across multiple organizational contexts — layoffs, pay decisions, performance appraisals, conflict resolution — and integrates them into a single theoretical framework. The interaction pattern is robust across laboratory experiments and field studies.

## Key Arguments

| Condition | Effect on Employee Reactions |
|---|---|
| Unfavorable outcome + fair procedure | Moderately negative reaction (procedure buffers the blow) |
| Unfavorable outcome + unfair procedure | Strongly negative reaction (worst case) |
| Favorable outcome + fair procedure | Strongly positive reaction (best case) |
| Favorable outcome + unfair procedure | Moderately positive reaction (outcome compensates) |

The interaction means that **procedural justice is most impactful precisely when organizations need it most** — during difficult decisions.

## Open Questions

- Does this interaction pattern hold across cultures, or is it specific to individualistic Western contexts?
- How do repeated negative outcomes (multiple rounds of layoffs) affect the buffering capacity of procedural fairness?
- Can organizations "bank" procedural fairness in good times to draw on during crises?
