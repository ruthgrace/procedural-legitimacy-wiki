**Authors:** Aaron Martin, Gosia Mikolajczak, and Raymond Orr

**Published:** 2020 (online) / 2022 (print), *International Political Science Review*, Vol. 43, No. 2

**Domain:** Government policymaking broadly (experimental study)

A foundational claim of procedural justice theory is that fair processes can make even unfavorable outcomes more acceptable. But does this claim hold in the domain of public policy? Martin, Mikolajczak, and Orr test it directly using survey experiments in which participants evaluate policy outcomes that were produced through either fair or unfair procedures. Their findings confirm that procedural arrangements are a significant factor in citizens' evaluations: policy made through procedurally fair processes is judged as more legitimate and more successful, even when the substantive outcome is held constant.

This is an important experimental confirmation of what much of the procedural legitimacy literature has argued on the basis of observational data. By holding outcomes constant and varying only the process, the authors can isolate the causal effect of procedural fairness on legitimacy judgments. The results suggest that the social psychology of procedural fairness — well-established in organizational and legal contexts — translates robustly to the public policy domain.

The practical implication is clear: how governments make decisions is not just a matter of democratic principle — it is a determinant of whether citizens accept those decisions as legitimate and evaluate them as successful. Governments that attend to process, not just outcomes, can build a reservoir of public acceptance that sustains policy implementation over time.

## Background

The study draws on experimental methods from political psychology and is situated within the broader debate about input legitimacy (the fairness of the process) versus output legitimacy (the quality of the outcome). The authors test whether input legitimacy has independent effects on policy evaluations, or whether outcomes dominate.

## Key Arguments

| Experimental Condition | Effect on Policy Evaluation |
|---|---|
| Fair process + favorable outcome | Highest legitimacy and success ratings |
| Fair process + unfavorable outcome | Higher legitimacy ratings than unfair process with same outcome |
| Unfair process + favorable outcome | Lower legitimacy ratings despite good outcome |
| Unfair process + unfavorable outcome | Lowest legitimacy and success ratings |

The key finding: procedural fairness has an independent, positive effect on policy legitimacy evaluations that is *not* reducible to the quality of the outcome.

## Open Questions

- How large is the procedural fairness effect relative to the outcome effect in high-stakes versus low-stakes policy domains?
- Do partisan or ideological priors moderate the effect of procedural fairness on policy evaluations?
- Can procedural fairness cues be manipulated or co-opted by governments seeking to legitimize unpopular policies?
