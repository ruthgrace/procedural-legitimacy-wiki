**Authors:** Laura Rivera and Dean Knox

**Published:** 2023, *American Political Science Review*

**Domain:** Bureaucratic discretion, equity, legitimacy, policy implementation

Rivera and Knox highlighted a fundamental tension in reform efforts: discretion can be a tool for both equity and inequity. In some contexts, bureaucrats use discretion to help marginalized populations navigate rigid systems — exercising flexibility that formal rules would not permit. A caseworker who helps an undocumented immigrant access emergency services, a teacher who bends attendance rules for a student facing homelessness, a parole officer who exercises leniency for a person addressing substance abuse — each uses discretion to promote equity in ways that rigid rule application would not allow.

Eliminating discretion in the name of consistency could therefore harm the very populations that reforms aim to protect. Rivera and Knox argue that the legitimacy of discretion depends not on its existence but on the direction and consistency of its exercise. Discretion used to promote equity — helping marginalized populations, addressing systemic barriers, tailoring policy to individual needs — can enhance legitimacy. Discretion used to reinforce existing advantages — favoring higher-status citizens, applying double standards, or exercising bias — undermines it.

For procedural legitimacy, this work complicates simple prescriptions. The standard procedural justice recommendation — apply rules consistently — can conflict with equity goals if the rules themselves are unjust or if their uniform application produces unequal outcomes. The challenge is to design institutions that channel discretion toward equity while constraining discriminatory discretion.

## Background

Rivera and Knox combine theoretical analysis with empirical evidence on how discretion operates in practice across various policy domains. Their work contributes to the growing literature on the relationship between bureaucratic discretion, equity, and democratic legitimacy.

## Key Arguments

| Concept | Description |
|---|---|
| Discretion for equity | Bureaucrats use flexibility to help marginalized populations navigate rigid rules |
| Discretion for inequity | The same flexibility can enable discrimination and favoritism |
| Elimination paradox | Removing discretion can harm the populations it's meant to protect |
| Direction matters | The legitimacy of discretion depends on whose interests it serves |
| Design challenge | Institutions must channel discretion toward equity while constraining discriminatory use |

## Open Questions

- How can institutions distinguish between equity-promoting and equity-undermining uses of discretion?
- Can structured discretion frameworks channel flexibility toward marginalized populations?
- How do bureaucrats themselves navigate the tension between consistency and equity?
