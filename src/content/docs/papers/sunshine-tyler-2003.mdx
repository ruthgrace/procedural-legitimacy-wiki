**Authors:** Jason Sunshine and Tom R. Tyler

**Published:** 2003, *Law & Society Review*, Vol. 37, pp. 513–548

**Domain:** Policing and public trust (New York City)

Why do people cooperate with the police? The deterrence model assumes the answer is fear of punishment, but Sunshine and Tyler present compelling evidence for a different mechanism: legitimacy. Using two surveys of New York City residents, they demonstrate that public willingness to cooperate with and support the police depends primarily on whether people view the police as a legitimate authority -- and that the key antecedent of perceived legitimacy is procedural fairness. When people believe the police make decisions fairly, treat people with dignity, and allow citizens voice, they are far more likely to view the police as legitimate, comply with police directives, cooperate as witnesses, and support police authority. This holds even after controlling for outcome favorability, crime rates, and demographic characteristics.

The study's most important finding concerns racial and ethnic differences. The effect of procedural justice on legitimacy is especially strong for racial and ethnic minorities. For communities that have historically experienced discriminatory policing, the quality of procedural treatment is the dominant factor shaping whether they view the police as a legitimate institution worthy of cooperation. This implies that procedural violations -- stops without explanation, disrespectful treatment, opaque decision-making -- disproportionately damage trust and cooperation among already-marginalized groups, compounding existing inequities. The practical implication is that police departments seeking to build public trust, particularly in communities of color, must prioritize procedural fairness over enforcement intensity.

## Background

The study draws on Tyler's broader theory of legitimacy and procedural justice, extending the framework from courtroom settings to everyday policing encounters. The two surveys sampled residents across New York City's diverse neighborhoods, enabling analysis of how procedural justice effects vary across racial, ethnic, and socioeconomic groups. The research was conducted in the early 2000s, a period of significant debate about policing strategies in New York City, including the "broken windows" and "stop and frisk" policies.

## Key Arguments

| Factor Tested | Effect on Legitimacy and Cooperation | Relative Importance |
|---|---|---|
| Procedural fairness (voice, respect, neutrality) | Strong positive effect on legitimacy and cooperation | Dominant predictor across all groups |
| Distributive fairness (outcome favorability) | Moderate effect | Weaker than procedural fairness |
| Performance (crime control effectiveness) | Modest effect | Less important than process quality |
| Racial/ethnic minority status | Amplifies the importance of procedural fairness | Procedural justice matters most for minorities |

## Open Questions

- How do body-worn cameras and other accountability technologies affect the procedural justice-legitimacy link in policing?
- Does the procedural justice effect hold similarly across different types of police encounters (traffic stops, emergency calls, community policing)?
- Can procedural justice training for officers produce measurable improvements in community trust, or are structural reforms also needed?
