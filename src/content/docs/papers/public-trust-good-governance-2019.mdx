**Authors:** Taye Demissie Beshi and Ranvinderjit Kaur

**Published:** 2020, *Public Organization Review*, Vol. 20, No. 2, pp. 337--350 (online 2019)

**Domain:** Local government trust and governance quality (Bahir Dar, Ethiopia)

What makes citizens trust their local government? Beshi and Kaur develop and test a model linking three pillars of good governance -- transparency, accountability, and responsiveness -- to public trust in a municipal administration. Using survey data from Bahir Dar City Administration in Ethiopia, they find that all three governance practices significantly predict citizens' trust, but with different magnitudes. Responsiveness emerges as the strongest predictor, followed by accountability and then transparency. The finding that *how government responds to citizens' needs* matters more than either openness or answerability suggests that procedural fairness is not just about information disclosure -- it is fundamentally about whether government acts on what it hears.

This study is valuable because it tests governance-trust relationships in a developing-country context where institutional capacity and public expectations differ markedly from the Western democracies that dominate the literature. The Ethiopian context, with its rapid urbanization and evolving local governance structures, provides a setting where good governance practices are not taken for granted, making citizens' evaluations of these practices particularly consequential for institutional legitimacy. The results reinforce the procedural justice literature's emphasis on voice and respect: citizens who feel their government listens and responds are more willing to trust it, regardless of the resource constraints under which it operates.

## Background

The study draws on the good governance literature that identifies transparency, accountability, and responsiveness as core dimensions of governance quality. Beshi and Kaur use a survey-based research design, collecting data from residents of Bahir Dar, the capital of the Amhara Region in Ethiopia. The conceptual model treats perceived governance practices as independent variables and public trust as the dependent variable, tested through regression analysis. The study connects to the broader procedural justice tradition by demonstrating that process-oriented governance attributes -- rather than service delivery outcomes alone -- shape citizens' trust evaluations at the local level.

## Open Questions

- Does the relative ordering of responsiveness > accountability > transparency hold in other developing-country municipal contexts, or is it specific to the Ethiopian governance environment?
- How do citizens form perceptions of governance practices in contexts with limited media coverage of local government operations?
- Can improvements in one governance dimension compensate for deficiencies in another, or do citizens evaluate them holistically?
