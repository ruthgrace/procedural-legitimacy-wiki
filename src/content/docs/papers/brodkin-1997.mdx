**Authors:** Evelyn Z. Brodkin

**Published:** 1997, *Social Service Review*, Vol. 71, No. 1, pp. 1–33

**Domain:** Welfare administration, street-level bureaucracy, policy implementation

[Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=Inside+the+Welfare+Contract%3A+Discretion+and+Accountability+in+State+Welfare+Administration)

Brodkin demonstrated how welfare bureaucrats exercised discretion in ways that systematically undermined formal policy goals. Through fieldwork in Chicago welfare offices, she showed that caseworkers — operating under heavy caseloads and organizational pressure — developed informal practices for processing cases that diverged significantly from official procedures. The discretion exercised at the front lines effectively created a different policy than the one enacted by legislators, with outcomes shaped by organizational convenience rather than client need.

Brodkin's key insight is that street-level discretion is not random but structured by organizational conditions. When caseworkers face impossible caseloads, they develop shortcuts: categorizing clients quickly, prioritizing easy-to-process cases, and using informal screening criteria that may have little to do with formal eligibility standards. These organizational adaptations systematically affect who receives benefits, how they are treated, and whether policy goals are achieved.

For procedural legitimacy, Brodkin's work shows that formal procedures may look fair on paper while being systematically undermined in practice. The procedural fairness citizens experience depends not on the rules as written but on how organizational conditions shape the discretion of the workers who implement those rules. A fair-sounding welfare policy can produce unfair outcomes when the conditions of implementation encourage or require caseworkers to exercise discretion in biased ways.

## Background

Brodkin conducted ethnographic research in Chicago welfare offices during the implementation of welfare reform. Her work drew on both Lipsky's street-level bureaucracy framework and organizational theory to explain how and why frontline practices diverged from policy intent. The research was particularly significant because it documented how welfare reform — which aimed to increase accountability — actually created new forms of unaccountable discretion.

## Key Arguments

| Finding | Implication |
|---|---|
| Informal practices diverge from formal rules | Policy as experienced differs from policy as designed |
| Organizational conditions structure discretion | Heavy caseloads, pressure, and resource scarcity shape how discretion is exercised |
| Discretion undermines policy goals | Caseworker shortcuts systematically affect who benefits from policy |
| Accountability paradox | Reforms aimed at accountability create new unaccountable practices |
| Organizational convenience over client need | Processing efficiency becomes the primary driver of decisions |

## Open Questions

- How can welfare systems be designed to align organizational incentives with policy goals and client needs?
- Does reducing caseloads meaningfully improve the quality of discretionary decisions?
- Can technology help identify when informal practices are systematically diverging from formal policy?
