**Authors:** Sean Young and Susannah Tanner

**Published:** 2023, *Public Administration Review*

**Domain:** Local governance, citizen participation, bureaucratic discretion, policy implementation

[Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=Bureaucratic+Discretion+and+Citizen+Participation)

Young and Tanner found that in practice, bureaucratic discretion dominates over citizen participation in shaping policy outcomes. Through a study of local governance, they showed that even in systems with formal participation mechanisms — public hearings, advisory boards, comment periods — the decisions that most affect citizens' lives are made through the exercise of bureaucratic discretion rather than through participatory processes. The participatory apparatus provides a sense of democratic input, but the real policy is made through the discretionary choices of bureaucrats and officials.

This finding challenges the assumption that participation reforms can, on their own, improve procedural legitimacy. If the meaningful decisions happen through bureaucratic discretion rather than through participatory channels, then improving the quality of participation will have limited impact unless it is accompanied by reforms that constrain and structure the discretion where real decisions are made. Young and Tanner argue that the focus on improving participation, while well-intentioned, can be a distraction from the more consequential issue of how discretion is exercised.

For procedural legitimacy, this work underscores the importance of focusing reform efforts where they will have the most impact. If bureaucratic discretion is the primary mechanism through which policy is implemented, then procedural legitimacy depends above all on how that discretion is structured, constrained, and held accountable.

## Background

The study examined local governance processes in multiple jurisdictions, comparing the outcomes of formal participatory processes with the outcomes of bureaucratic discretionary decisions. The researchers tracked how policy decisions were actually made and how they related to citizen input.

## Key Arguments

| Finding | Implication |
|---|---|
| Discretion dominates participation | Real policy outcomes are shaped more by bureaucratic choices than citizen input |
| Formal participation ≠ influence | Participatory mechanisms exist but have limited effect on outcomes |
| Focus mismatch | Reform efforts focus on participation while discretion drives outcomes |
| Discretion as primary mechanism | Procedural legitimacy depends most on how discretion is structured |
| Participation as symbolic | Participatory processes may provide legitimacy cover without substantive influence |

## Open Questions

- Under what conditions does citizen participation meaningfully constrain bureaucratic discretion?
- Can participatory mechanisms be redesigned to have genuine influence over discretionary decisions?
- Does the dominance of discretion over participation vary across policy domains?
