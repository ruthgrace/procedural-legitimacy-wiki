**Authors:** E. Allan Lind and Christiane Arndt

**Published:** 2016, OECD Regulatory Policy Working Papers, No. 6, OECD Publishing, Paris

**Domain:** Permitting, licensing, regulatory compliance, and everyday government-citizen interactions

[Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=Perceived+Fairness+and+Regulatory+Policy%3A+A+Behavioural+Science+Perspective+on+Government-Citizen+Interactions)

This OECD working paper is one of the most practically oriented contributions to the procedural legitimacy literature. Lind and Arndt synthesize decades of behavioral science research on procedural fairness and apply it directly to the kinds of government encounters most citizens actually have: applying for permits, complying with regulations, dealing with licensing requirements, and navigating bureaucratic processes. Their central argument is that three elements — voice, respect, and explanations — are the primary drivers of perceived fairness in these interactions, and that perceived fairness in turn drives compliance, trust, and cooperation.

The paper is significant because it bridges the gap between academic procedural justice theory and practical regulatory design. Rather than studying fairness perceptions as an abstract variable, Lind and Arndt offer concrete recommendations for how government agencies can redesign their processes to increase perceived fairness. These include providing clear explanations for decisions, giving citizens opportunities to present their case before decisions are made, treating people with dignity in all interactions, and being transparent about how decisions are reached.

For local governments specifically, the paper's framework applies directly to building departments, planning offices, licensing bureaus, and any front-counter or online interaction where citizens encounter the administrative state. The recommendations are actionable at the city and county level without requiring legislative change.

## Background

Lind is one of the originators of the group value model of procedural justice (with Tyler), and this paper represents a late-career effort to translate that body of research into practical policy guidance. The OECD context means the paper is written for policymakers rather than academics, making it unusually accessible.

## Key Arguments

| Fairness Driver | Description | Example in Local Government |
|---|---|---|
| Voice | Citizens have a chance to tell their side before a decision is made | Applicant can explain their project before a permit is denied |
| Respect | Citizens are treated with dignity and courtesy | Building inspector treats homeowner as a partner, not a suspect |
| Explanations | Decisions are accompanied by clear rationale | Zoning denial letter explains which code section applies and why |
| Transparency | The process and criteria are visible and understandable | Permit application webpage clearly lists requirements and timeline |
| Consistency | Similar cases are treated similarly | Two similar home renovation projects are evaluated by the same standards |

## Open Questions

- How do resource-constrained local governments implement these recommendations without slowing down already lengthy permit processes?
- Do digital/online interactions (e-permitting, online applications) make it harder or easier to deliver voice and respect?
- How do frontline government employees respond to training in procedural fairness principles?
