**Authors:** Rick L. Lawrence, Steven E. Daniels, and George H. Stankey

**Published:** 1997, *Society & Natural Resources*, Vol. 10, No. 6, pp. 577–589

**Domain:** Public hearings, participatory decision-making, natural resource management

Public hearings are one of the most common ways local governments solicit citizen input — on everything from forest management plans to park budgets to development proposals. But do these hearings actually make people feel the process is fair? Lawrence, Daniels, and Stankey apply procedural justice theory from social psychology to public involvement in natural resource decision-making, and their findings suggest that many standard public participation formats fall short. The problem is not that participation is offered, but that it often lacks the key ingredients that procedural justice research identifies as essential: genuine voice, perceived influence over the outcome, and inclusive access.

The authors review the public involvement literature and find that while agencies cite many reasons for involving the public (legal requirements, information gathering, conflict resolution), there has been surprisingly little systematic attention to whether the procedures themselves are perceived as fair by participants. They argue that procedural justice theory offers a rigorous framework for designing and evaluating public involvement processes — one that goes beyond simply checking the box of "we held a hearing."

For local governments, the paper's message is directly relevant to planning commission meetings, city council hearings, community workshops, and any process where citizen input is solicited. The key insight is that the *design* of the participation process — not just its existence — determines whether citizens experience it as fair.

## Background

The paper draws on Thibaut and Walker's (1975) foundational work on process control and outcome control, as well as Tyler and Lind's group value model. It bridges social psychology and natural resource management, but the procedural principles apply to any local government participatory process.

## Key Arguments

| Procedural Element | What It Means | Common Failure Mode |
|---|---|---|
| Voice | Participants can express their views and concerns | Public comment periods too short or too late in the process |
| Influence | Participant input can actually affect the decision | Decisions are effectively made before the hearing |
| Inclusiveness | All affected parties have access to the process | Hearings held at times/locations that exclude working families |
| Information | Participants have access to relevant information | Technical documents are inaccessible to non-experts |
| Consistency | The process applies the same standards to all cases | Some stakeholders get private access to decision-makers |

## Open Questions

- How can local governments redesign public hearings to deliver genuine voice and influence rather than performative participation?
- Do newer participation formats (citizen assemblies, participatory budgeting, online engagement) perform better on procedural justice criteria?
- How do power imbalances between organized interests and individual residents affect perceived fairness of public processes?
