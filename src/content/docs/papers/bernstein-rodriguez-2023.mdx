**Authors:** Alissa Bernstein and María Rodriguez

**Published:** 2023, *Journal of Public Administration Research and Theory*, Vol. 33, No. 3, pp. 442–456

**Domain:** Street-level bureaucracy, accountability mechanisms, public management

[Google Scholar](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=How+Street-Level+Bureaucrats+Are+Held+Accountable)

Bernstein and Rodriguez examined the actual mechanisms through which frontline workers face consequences for their discretionary decisions. They found that accountability operates through multiple channels — supervisory oversight, peer norms, client complaints, legal requirements, and professional standards — and that these channels interact in complex ways. The effectiveness of accountability depends not on any single mechanism but on the overall configuration of oversight structures.

A key finding is that excessive accountability pressure can itself be counterproductive. When workers face intense scrutiny from multiple directions, they prioritize documentation over service quality, exercise discretion in ways that minimize personal risk rather than serve client interests, and develop defensive routines that undermine the very outcomes accountability is supposed to promote. This "accountability overload" produces a form of bureaucratic behavior that looks accountable on paper but fails to deliver fair, responsive service.

For procedural legitimacy, this research suggests that the relationship between accountability and fairness is not linear. Some accountability pressure improves procedural fairness by constraining arbitrary discretion; too much accountability pressure undermines fairness by making bureaucrats defensive and risk-averse. The design challenge is finding the right configuration of accountability mechanisms that constrain harmful discretion without stifling beneficial flexibility.

## Background

The study examined frontline workers across multiple public service settings, combining interviews, observations, and analysis of organizational accountability structures. The researchers drew on both the street-level bureaucracy literature and the growing body of work on accountability in public organizations.

## Key Arguments

| Finding | Implication |
|---|---|
| Multiple accountability channels | Oversight, peers, clients, law, and professional norms all constrain discretion |
| Configuration matters | No single mechanism is sufficient; the overall system determines effectiveness |
| Accountability overload | Excessive pressure produces defensive, documentation-focused behavior |
| Risk aversion | Over-accountable workers prioritize self-protection over client service |
| Non-linear relationship | More accountability does not always mean better outcomes |

## Open Questions

- What is the optimal configuration of accountability mechanisms for different types of street-level work?
- Can organizational design reduce accountability overload while maintaining meaningful oversight?
- How do workers themselves perceive the balance between helpful constraint and counterproductive pressure?
